Zephyr - Fully 3D Printed Aircraft

My latest project is a new iteration of a fully 3D-printed aircraft, built by applying everything I learned from the SAROS project below.

The Zephyr is a 1.2 m twin-motor aircraft designed in Solidworks to improve performance while pushing my CAD skills further. It shares a lot of DNA with the SAROS, while adopting a blended-body design.

Compared to the SAROS, the Zephyr is more transportable because its wings are easy to remove without exposed wires. All connections needed to power the motors and control surfaces are integrated directly into the 3D-printed structure.

The Zephyr has not flown yet, but it is fully built and scheduled for its maiden flight in the next few weeks.

Internet-Controlled UAV System

Objective: Enable real-time control of UAVs from anywhere in the world using an internet connection.

Developed a system integrating a companion computer (Raspberry Pi) to bridge flight controller telemetry through secure VPN networks. Enabled long-distance communication between the UAV and ground station.

Implemented live video streaming and low-latency control using MAVLink, WebRTC, and ground station software, ensuring stable communication over global distances.

Successfully conducted long-distance tests over ~13,000 km (Dubai to Dallas), demonstrating real-time control feasibility across continents. The system is currently under active development.

ARISE - Aerial Robotics Capstone Competition - April 2024

Roles: Team captain, Technical Lead on payload design

Developed a payload system designed to deliver a total of 100 balls to 4 distinct targets. Created 4 lightweight fabric arms, each carrying 25 balls, which rotated downward for efficient release. Each arm featured a locking flap at the end that opened automatically based on orientation. Each arm was powered by a 35 kg high-torque servo, coupled with a 3D-printed gear mechanism, allowing the arms to rotate between 90 and 100 degrees.

Additionally, I built custom Li-ion batteries to extend range without added weight, assembling a 4S2P - 116.8V 8400mAh pack using 21700 Molicel P42A cells.

Documented all technical specifications in a comprehensive 57-page report.

School project completed in 10th grade. This solar-powered Arduino boat is capable of autonomously collecting debris from the water surface using a net and rake. It can also record atmospheric data.

How I made it: The main structure is built from 20x20 aluminum extrusions, supported by 2 floaters made of 6" PVC pipes, and propelled by 2 underwater propellers that steer using differential thrust. All electronics are protected in 2 separate waterproof plastic boxes: one containing the Arduino, receiver, and power distribution, and the other housing 2 6V lead batteries connected in series, along with the solar-powered MPPT.

For many of my UAVs (drones, fixed wings, and Capstone project), I built and used custom-assembled Li-ion batteries. These batteries are more energy-dense than the commonly used LiPo technology, providing UAVs with extended flight time. Additionally, at equal capacity, these custom-made batteries are more economical than off-the-shelf options.

Building these batteries required precision and attention to avoid wiring errors. The downside of Li-ion batteries is their lower power output (Amps), which provides less thrust and doesn’t handle rapid power surges well. For the Capstone drone, I made two 4S2P packs (photos below) and connected them in parallel to increase the current from 45A to 90A, effectively doubling the capacity.
